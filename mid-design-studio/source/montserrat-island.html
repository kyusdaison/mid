An Island Like No Other

Montserrat lies in the Lesser Antilles chain, approximately 27 miles southwest of Antigua and 30 miles northwest of Guadeloupe. Known affectionately as the "Emerald Isle," the nickname reflects both its lush, mountainous landscape and its deep Irish heritage.

Irish Roots, Caribbean Soul

Colonized in 1632 by Irish Catholics from nearby Saint Kitts, Montserrat is the only place outside Ireland to declare St. Patrick's Day an official public holiday. The island's culture weaves together Irish, African, and Caribbean traditions into something utterly unique.

Shaped by Fire

The 1995 eruption of the Soufrière Hills volcano transformed the island forever. The southern two-thirds — including the capital Plymouth — became an exclusion zone. Today, Montserrat is a tale of two halves: the vibrant, green north and the ashen, haunting south.

Through the Ages
A Rich & Dramatic History
From ancient settlements to volcanic rebirth, Montserrat's story is one of resilience.
~3000 BCE
First Inhabitants
The earliest known inhabitants — Amerindian peoples — arrived on the island, leaving behind traces of an ancient civilization.
1493
Columbus Sights the Island
Christopher Columbus spotted the island during his second voyage and named it Santa María de Montserrate after the abbey in Spain.
1632
Irish Catholic Settlement
Irish Catholics from nearby Saint Kitts established the first European settlement, beginning a unique cultural legacy that endures today.
1768
St. Patrick's Day Uprising
An attempted slave revolt on March 17th is now commemorated alongside Irish heritage during St. Patrick's Week celebrations.
1995
Soufrière Hills Erupts
After centuries of dormancy, the volcano roared back to life, beginning a series of eruptions that would reshape the island's geography and future.
1997
The Great Evacuation
A devastating eruption killed 19 people and buried the capital Plymouth in ash. The southern two-thirds of the island became a permanent exclusion zone.
Today
Resilience & Renewal
Montserrat thrives in the north, with a new capital being built at Little Bay. The island markets itself as the "Pompeii of the Caribbean," drawing visitors to its unique story.

The island's only white sand beach, accessible by boat or a strenuous coastal hike. Crystal-clear waters make it perfect for swimming, diving, and snorkeling.

Centre Hills Rainforest

A primeval tropical rainforest rising to over 700m, recognized as one of the Caribbean's most biodiverse birding areas. Home to Antillean crested hummingbirds and rare forest thrushes.

Biodiversity
Remarkable Wildlife
A small island with an extraordinary array of species.

Home to the "Mountain Chicken"

Despite its name, the "mountain chicken" is actually a critically endangered giant ditch frog — one of the world's rarest amphibians. Montserrat is one of only two places on Earth where it survives.

The island also hosts a remarkable diversity of invertebrates, with over 1,200 documented species, and its Centre Hills rainforest is a globally important bird area.

Digital Future
Digital Residency Programme
Montserrat is pioneering a premium digital residency ecosystem — anchored in legislation, powered by blockchain, and designed for global professionals.

Under the proposed Digital Residency Bill 2025, Montserrat is building a high-assurance digital identity platform targeted at vetted global professionals, entrepreneurs, and digital founders.

Unlike low-friction, high-volume digital ID programmes elsewhere, Montserrat's approach prioritises quality over quantity — using premium pricing, multi-year commitments, and rigorous due diligence to build a credible, bank-usable credential backed by British Overseas Territory legal standards.

Zero-Capex Model

Partnered with the Future Citizen Bureau (FCB), the government bears no upfront financial risk. FCB funds all platform development, compliance operations, security, and global marketing through a revenue-sharing arrangement.

Legislative Framework

The Bill establishes a Digital Residency Office managed by a Director, with mandatory background checks through the Financial Intelligence Unit (FIU). All applicant data is encrypted and blockchain-authenticated, ensuring sovereign data control.

Travel
Plan Your Visit
Everything you need to know before traveling to the Emerald Isle.

✈️ Getting There

  • Fly via Antigua (V.C. Bird International Airport)
  • Short connecting flight to John A. Osborne Airport
  • Ferry service from Antigua available
  • No direct flights from North America or Europe

🗓️ Best Time to Visit

The dry season from December to April is ideal, with temperatures between 25–28°C. Hurricane season runs June through November, though Montserrat is relatively sheltered.

💰 Currency & Language

  • Official currency: East Caribbean Dollar (XCD)
  • US dollars widely accepted
  • Official language: English
  • Local Creole also spoken

⚠️ Exclusion Zone

The southern part of the island remains restricted due to volcanic activity. Guided tours are available subject to seismic conditions. Always follow local guidance and respect restricted areas.

🏨 Where to Stay

Accommodation ranges from cozy guesthouses and villas to eco-lodges. Most options are in the northern part of the island around Salem, Olveston, and Woodlands.

🍽️ Local Flavours

  • Goat water (national dish — hearty goat stew)
  • Fresh seafood and Caribbean cuisine
  • Rum punch and local spirits
  • St. Patrick's celebrations feature traditional food
